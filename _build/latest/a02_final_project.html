Kaggle Competitions#

What is Kaggle?#

Kaggle is a platform primarily for predictive modeling and analytics competitions. Individuals and teams can participate in real-world challenges posted by organizations, where the primary goal is often to produce the best models for predictive data analysis.

What is a Kaggle competition?#

Kaggle competitions are online contests hosted on the Kaggle platform where data scientists and enthusiasts tackle real-world problems using machine learning, with top solutions often earning prizes and recognition. Participants train their models on provided datasets, submit predictions, and are ranked on a public leaderboard based on their model’s accuracy or other predefined metrics.

  • List of Kaggle Competitions

Structure of a Kaggle Competition#

  • Problem Statement: Organizations post a detailed problem statement and provide relevant datasets.

  • Dataset: Participants are given a training dataset, with which they can train their machine learning models, and a test dataset, with which they can predict outcomes.

  • Submission & Leaderboard: After making predictions on the test set, participants can submit their results to Kaggle, which then evaluates the results based on a predefined metric and ranks participants on a public leaderboard.

  • Prizes: Many competitions offer cash prizes for the top finishers, but the competition isn’t just about the money. Being highly ranked in a Kaggle competition can lead to job opportunities and significant recognition in the data science community.

Types of Competitions#

  • Featured Competitions: These are high-profile competitions with cash prizes. They’re often sponsored by large organizations seeking innovative solutions to complex problems.

  • Research Competitions: These competitions don’t typically have cash prizes but allow participants to work on unique, cutting-edge problems.

  • Getting Started Competitions: For those new to data science and Kaggle, these competitions are a great place to learn and practice without the pressure of competing for a prize.

  • Playground Competitions: These are more casual and can cover a wide range of topics, providing a fun way to experiment and learn.

Beyond Competitions#

While Kaggle is best known for its competitions, it’s also a broader platform for data science with features like Kaggle Kernels (now called “Notebooks”) – a place where users can write and share their code, datasets, and analyses – and Kaggle Datasets, a vast repository of datasets for various uses.

Kaggle also boasts a vibrant community forum where participants discuss competitions, share techniques, and seek collaborations. Additionally, Kaggle provides learning resources, including courses on various data science topics.

In Conclusion#

Kaggle competitions provide a real-world, hands-on opportunity for data enthusiasts, analysts, and scientists to hone their skills, learn from peers, and potentially earn prizes and recognition. For businesses and researchers, they offer a way to tap into the collective intelligence of a global community to solve specific challenges.

Project Requirements#

  • Students collaborate in teams, each comprising 3 to 4 members.

  • Each team will get a random presentation data from the slots.

  • Each group participates in any one of the Kaggle competitions.

    • The whole Competition List

    • The Competitions filtered by Getting Started (such as Spaceship Titanic)

    • The Competitions filtered by Playground (such as San Francisco Crime Classification.

    • Or any other competitions

      • Please mainly focus on the tabular data analysis.

      • The competitions without specific evaluation metrics are NOT allowed, such as NFL Big Data Bowl 2022.

  • How to choose a competitions:

    • Your project should primarily involve classification, regression, dimension reduction, or clustering. These are the areas we’ve explored in depth, and I believe you are equipped in these domains.

    • Consider the complexity of the dataset and the problem statement. Ensure that it’s manageable within the timeframe and resources available to you.

    • To ensure that your selected competition aligns with our course objectives, please submit a brief description (as Deliverable 1) of the Kaggle competition you wish to pursue. I will review your submissions and provide feedback or suggestions for adjustments if necessary.

  • You must try several different methods to solve the problem. The method you finally choose for the competition does not have to be a model taught in this course.

  • You must compare your fancy methods with simple baselines, e.g., random guess, all-positive, all-negative, simple linear models, and beat the baselines. The evaluation metric must be the one required by the competition.

Milestone and Final Delivery#

Note: Each deliverable in this project is interconnected. Missing or delaying one deliverable can significantly impact your ability to successfully complete subsequent deliverables and also impact your grades.

Deliverable 1: project proposal (10%)#

  • Students work as teams.

  • Each team needs to register for a Kaggle competition.

  • Submit your proposal, including competition name, team members, overview, relevance to this course, and plan on Canvas.

  • Proposal template

Instructor will review and provide feedback on your proposals.

Deliverable 2: dummy model checkpoint (10%)#

  • For this milestone, you will need to have downloaded the data, and also run some initial pre-processing on it.

  • You should also make at least one dummy submission on Kaggle.

  • You submit the following results on Canvas.

    • the Jupyter Notebook for the pre-processing and dummy models (60 pts).

    • a screenshot of scores for the dummy submissions at Kaggle (40 pts).

Note: a dummy submission is the one where the prediction is meaningless. For example, the predictions are randomly-assigned, always-positive, always-negative, or most-frequency

Deliverable 3: checkpoint of advanced models (10%)#

For this milestone, you

  • should do more data preprocessing and have made at least two non-dummy submissions on Kaggle.

  • You submit the following results on Canvas.

    • the Jupyter Notebook including the non-dummy models (60 pts).

    • a screenshot of scores for the non-dummy submissions at Kaggle (40 pts).

Deliverable 4: presentation (40%)#

  • Give a presentation and show your explanation and results obtained by your presentation date.

  • The complete results are expected in the final report.

  • Please submit your presentation slides (pptx or pdf) on Cavnas before your presentation. Instructor will download them to the class computer.

Presentation Grading criteria

Deliverable 5: report (30%)#

By this time, you should

  • have made at least six non-dummy submissions with different settings (models, preprocessing, hyper-parameters) on Kaggle totally.

  • submit the Jupyter Notebook as the final report (including implementation, documentation, explanation…).

  • you should clearly explain what experiments (data preprocessing, feature engineering, machine learning models) you have conducted in the Jupyter Notebook.

Presentation#

We will host the presentation sessions at the end of this semester. The following is a suggested structure for the presentation. You don’t necessarily have to organize your presentation using these sections in this order, but that would likely be a good starting point for most projects.

  • Briefly describe the competition your group is tackling.

  • Describe the overall objective.

  • Introduce the dataset as well as the exploratory data analysis.

  • Data Preprocessing and Feature Engineering

  • Machine learning models including the dummy and non-dummy approaches you have tried.

  • Evaluation results.

  • Conclusion and future work

Students from Charleston will give the presentation online.

Tips#

  1. Start from a simple task, a simple model, and simple hyper-parameters.

  2. A good prediction performance is desired, but the workflow of machine learning is more important.

  3. This video is a tour for Kaggle.

  4. You can directly write your code at Kaggle and submit results. Please see the tutorials Getting Started on Kaggle: Python coding in Kernels and Getting Started on Kaggle: Writing code to analyze a dataset.

  5. You can also download the dataset and implement machine learning models offline. Then, you submit the prediction only. See the tutorial My First Kaggle Submission.

  6. There is a tutorial for starting a new Kaggle project: Beginner Kaggle Data Science Project Walk-Through (Titanic).

What if I want to do other topics#

For students wishing to delve into areas outside of the prescribed Kaggle competitions, you are encouraged to explore a topic of your personal interest. However, it is imperative that your chosen topic aligns with the course’s objectives and scope. To ensure this alignment and to allow for appropriate planning, please email a one-page summary to the instructor for review by Oct. 20, 2023.

Your one-page summary must address the following points:

  1. Topic Selection:

    • Definition: Clearly define the topic you intend to explore.

    • Objective: What specific problem or question does it address?

  1. Relevance to the Course:

    • Connection: Elaborate on how the topic connects with the course’s content and objectives.

    • Value: Why is it a valuable area of exploration in the context of our studies?

  2. Methodology:

    • Approach: Discuss the methodologies, algorithms, or techniques you plan to employ.

    • Justification: Provide a brief overview of why these methods are appropriate for your chosen topic.

  3. Expected Outputs:

    • Deliverables: List the milestone deliverables you anticipate over the course of the project. This can include datasets, visualizations, models, code, or any other pertinent results.

  1. Other Course Usage:

    • Transparency: Be transparent about whether this topic, or any components of it, has been or will be used in any other academic course. This is to ensure originality and prevent potential academic integrity issues.

Once your summary is submitted, it will be reviewed, and feedback will be provided. Please note that your topic must receive approval before proceeding. Any projects undertaken without prior approval may not be considered for grading and result zero for this portion.
